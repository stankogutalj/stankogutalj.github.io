General Overview

In a naturally aspirated internal combustion (IC) engine, air is supplied to the engine through the air intake manifold. The mechanism that drives the air into the cylinders is the pressure differential between the positive ambient pressure and negative cylinder pressure that is created when the piston moves toward the bottom of the intake stroke. In order to produce an efficient combustion there needs to be a steady mixture of air and fuel, commonly known as the air-to-fuel mixture, and if an increase in work is desired, the ratio of air to fuel needs to be increased. However, if too much air is added to the mix, the engine will be starved of fuel. In order to proportionately supply air and fuel to the engine so that an optimum condition will be achieved, the intake manifold is often tuned to complement the periodicity of the cylinder motion.
